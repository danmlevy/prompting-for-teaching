Pre-work module

Prompting for AI in Teaching

This short module will help you arrive ready to use AI tools more effectively during the program. You will learn a simple framework, write one teaching-related prompt, get feedback from an AI Prompt Coach, and revise it once.

⏱ 20 minutes required 🚀 Up to 45 minutes if you want to go deeper 🎯 Goal: improve, not perfect
1. Why This Matters

Good AI use starts with clear communication

AI tools can help with many teaching tasks: improving a syllabus, generating examples, designing activities, supporting students, creating simulations, or building a reusable teaching assistant.

But the quality of the output depends heavily on how clearly we communicate what we want.

In this module, you will practice writing a prompt that gives AI enough direction to be genuinely useful for your teaching.

Key idea: Good prompting is usually iterative. You do not need to write a perfect prompt on the first try. You write, test, get feedback, revise, and improve.
2. A Simple Framework

TIC: Task, Instructions, Context

For this module, we will use a simple structure called TIC. It is not the only way to write prompts, but it is a practical way to make your requests clearer.

T = Task

What do you want the AI to do?

Example: “Give me feedback on this assignment prompt.”

I = Instructions

How should the AI do it?

Example: “Focus on clarity, student motivation, and likely points of confusion.”

C = Context

What does the AI need to know?

Example: “This is for first-year MBA students with mixed prior experience.”

A useful prompt often answers these questions:

  • What am I trying to accomplish?
  • Who are the learners?
  • What should the AI pay attention to?
  • What should the AI avoid doing?
  • What kind of output would be most useful to me?
3. How Your Prompt Will Be Coached

What the Prompt Coach will look for

Before you write your prompt, it helps to know what kind of feedback you will receive.

The Prompt Coach will not simply judge whether your prompt is “good” or “bad.” It will help you make one practical improvement.

CriterionQuestion to ask yourself
Task clarityIs it clear what I want the AI to produce or help me think through?
Specific instructionsHave I told the AI how to approach the task, not just what the task is?
Useful contextHave I included enough information about the course, students, teaching goal, or constraints?
Teaching alignmentWould the output help me improve learning, not just generate generic content?
GuardrailsHave I told the AI what to avoid, especially if students might use the output?
UsabilityHave I specified the format or level of detail that would make the output easy to use?
Remember: Your first draft does not need to satisfy every criterion. The point is to see how your prompt can improve after feedback.
4. Choose Your Path

Select the task that best fits your current AI experience

Everyone will practice the same underlying skill: communicating clearly with AI. Choose the path that feels most useful to you.

Path A: Beginner

Improve something you already teach

Write a prompt asking AI to give feedback on a syllabus, assignment, case discussion plan, class activity, or lecture outline.

Good if: you are still building confidence with AI tools.

Path B: Intermediate

Design an AI teaching assistant

Write a prompt for an AI assistant that could help you generate examples, discussion questions, quiz questions, role plays, or student support materials.

Good if: you already use AI sometimes and want more teaching-specific value.

Path C: Advanced

Draft instructions for a reusable teaching bot

Write instructions for an AI tutor, coach, simulator, or feedback assistant that could be reused by you or your students.

Good if: you are ready to think about custom GPTs, projects, guardrails, and repeated use.

5. Draft Your Prompt

Write a first version

Use the TIC framework to write a teaching-related prompt. Do not worry about making it perfect. Make it clear enough that the Prompt Coach can help you improve it.

Optional starter structure:

Task: I want you to... Instructions: Please focus on... Please avoid... Please format the output as... Context: This is for... My students are... The teaching goal is... One constraint is...

6. Get Feedback from the Prompt Coach

Submit your prompt, then revise once

Paste your draft prompt into the Prompt Coach. The coach will help you identify what is already working and suggest one or two improvements.

  1. Paste your prompt into the Prompt Coach.
  2. Read the feedback.
  3. Revise your prompt once.
  4. If you have time, ask the coach: “What is one more advanced improvement I could make?”
